Part II: Math and Random

Fortunately, 'i' is no longer an illegal vowel. But unfortunately, you are now the administrator of the District 12 lottery. Every day, you must generate a random integer between 1 and 10 (inclusive). You then allow five people to buy a lottery ticket; they must let the lottery machine pick their play. If a ticket matches the daily pick, the holder gets a $5 jackpot (yes, if there are multiple winning tickerts, you're in the hole. I told you this was an unfortunate situation...). As administrator, you are not only interested in making sure jackpots are paid, but you also want to make sure that the general behavior of the system is working in your favor. Thus, every day, you calculate the mean and standard deviation of the randomly chosen ticket values. Write a short program that "runs" a day: it picks the winning number, it prints five random tickets, it determines the day's payout, and reports the mean and standard deviation of the picks.

By the way, the mean of n numbers (in case you didn't know) is the sum of the numbers divided by n (in our case, yes, five). The standard deviation is easily calculated once you know the mean: find each number's difference from the mean, square that difference, sum those squares, divide by n-1, and take the square root (if you prefer a formuler, this should turn one up). Your program should print doubles with no more than 2 numbers after the decimal point.
